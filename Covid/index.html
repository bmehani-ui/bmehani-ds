Introduction

The COVID-19 pandemic exposed many vulnerabilities in Americas prison system, especially regarding outbreak control and how they spread through certain correctional facilities. While some institutions reported only a few cases, others became major hotspots. This raises important questions about what factors contributed to the difference in these outcomes, particularly whether the type of facility played a role in the total number of reported inmate cases.

With this in mind, I investigate the question: Is there a relationship between the type of facility and the average number of inmate COVID-19 cases? I explore how various facility types, such as federal prisons, state prisons, and others, compare in terms of case counts and whether certain types are associated with higher case numbers.

I found strong evidence that the type of facility is associated with differences in the average number of inmate COVID-19 cases.

Background

Data Sources

The data comes from the New York Times. It was collected from websites that were overseen by state/federal prison systems, and there was also data from Immigration and Customs Enforcement. Not publicly available data was retrieved through direct requests and inquiries by NYT. The information was collected from 2,805 facilities from March 2020 to March 2021.

  • Data Link: https://github.com/nytimes/covid-19-data/blob/master/prisons/facilities.csv

Variables and Data Structure

In terms of variables, I used facility type, population, and inmate cases.

  • Facility type is the type of jail/prison that specific location is. This could be state or federal prisons, immigration detention centers, juvenile detention facilities, or local jails.

  • The population variable is the highest number of inmates that were at the specific facility from March 2020 to March 2021. For the population variable, we renamed it to total_pop and summed it up into one category for each type of facility.

  • The cases variable is the number of positively-tested COVID cases during that time period and it is also totaled for each type of facility.

  • The infection rate variable is the rate of COVID in each facility type. We calculated this column by dividing the inmate cases by the total population of each facility type (population).

  • Each row of our cleaned dataset shows a type of facility and its total population, inmate population, inmate cases, and infection rate in that singular facility.

  • The sample size is 12, which represents the total amount of facility types.

Statistical Analysis

This study investigates the difference in COVID-19 infection rates between federal prisons and jails. We will conduct a two-tailed hypothesis test because we have no prior reason to suspect a difference in the probability of getting infected in a jail vs in a federal prison.

Parameters of Interest

Our parameters of interest are the true probabilities of an inmate getting infected in jails vs in federal prisons.

  • p1 = probability of an inmate being infected in a federal prison
  • p2= probability of an inmate being infected in a jail

Type of Inference

We will be computing a difference of proportions.

Assumptions

  • X1 = the observed proportion of infected federal prison inmates to total federal prison population ~ Binom(137905, p1), n1 = 137905

  • X2 = the observed proportion of infected jail inmates to total jail population ~ Binom(146803, p2), n2 = 146803

Independence: It is assumed that the groups (jail population and federal prison population) are independent from each other. This assumption is satisfied as the data was collected from separate groups

Normality: It is assumed that the sample mean of each group is approximately normally distributed.

BINS Assumptions: It is assumed that BINS is all true.

Hypotheses

  • H_0: p1 = p2, p1 - p2 = 0

  • H_a: p1 \(\neq\) p2, p1 - p2 \(\neq\) 0

Test Statistic and Null Distribution

Test statistic is calculated as:

  • Test statistic is calculated as: ((p1hat - p2hat) - 0) / sqrt((pbar(1-pbar)) / n1) + (pbar(1-pbar)) / n2) ~ N(0, 1) (null distribution)

  • Where pbar = (X1 + X2)/(n1 + n2)

Results

  • Test Statistic: 40.39949

  • P-val = 0 (output rounded to 0 in R)

Interpretation

P-val < alpha = 0.05, so we reject the null hypothesis since we have evidence that the infection rate in federal prisons is higher than the infection rate in jails.

Discussion

Further Interpretation

The results of this analysis show that the p-value (0) is less than the significance level of 0.05. This provides strong evidence that we reject the null hypothesis and conclude that there is a significant relationship between the type of facility and the average number of inmate COVID-19 cases when analyzing infection rates in federal prisons and jails.

For readers without a background in statistics, the p-value represents the probability of observing such a difference (or a more extreme one) in infection rates if the true infection rates in jails and federal prisons were actually the same. In our analysis, the p-value was approximately zero, meaning that it is extremely unlikely the observed difference happened by chance. In other words, the data provides strong evidence that the infection rate in federal prisons is higher than in jails. Furthermore, the test statistic was very large (approximately 40.399), which reflects a substantial difference in the observed proportions and aligns with our one-sided hypothesis test. Taken together, these results strongly suggest that inmates in federal prisons faced a significantly higher risk of COVID-19 infection compared to those in jails.

The combination of the extremely small p-value and a very large test statistic provides strong evidence of a difference in COVID-19 infection rates between jails and federal prisons, with federal prisons showing significantly higher infection rates.

Shortcomings of the Analysis

Unaccounted Variables: The analysis is based solely on the number of reported COVID-19 cases within the facilities. This means factors such as inmate population size, facility capacity, or testing availability were not considered. These variables could have an impact on reported infection rates because, for example, a larger facility with more testing may report more cases, not necessarily because COVID-19 had spread more throughout the facility, but because there were more opportunities to report cases.

Time Window: The data may reflect a single time period during the pandemic, which limits our ability to understand how infection rates may have changed over time or in response to certain health measures.

Facility Policies: Differences in policies, such as isolation protocols, vaccine distribution speed, or visitation restrictions were not included in the data set. These policies could vary widely between jails and federal prisons and may have had a significant effect on infection spread.
